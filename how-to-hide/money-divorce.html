Is Hiding Assets During Divorce Legal in New York?

The Legal Obligation of Full Financial Disclosure

New York adheres to the principle of equitable distribution, which means that marital property should be divided fairly, though not necessarily equally. To ensure fairness, both spouses must outline their financial standing, including income, bank accounts, investments, real estate, and debts. This process is known as financial disclosure and is typically documented through a sworn statement of net worth.

Attempting to manipulate this process by underreporting assets, undervaluing property, or hiding income violates court rules and can lead to serious repercussions. Trying to figure out how to hide money divorce is not just a breach of trust—it can also lead to legal penalties that worsen one’s position during the proceedings.

Common Methods People Use to Conceal Assets

Though it is illegal, there are unfortunately several methods individuals may try to employ to hide assets during a divorce. These include:

  • Transferring funds to friends or family members under false pretenses
  • Creating secret bank accounts or using offshore investments
  • Purchasing valuable items and then underreporting their existence or value
  • Falsifying business records to reduce the income or profit made

Each of these tactics can be detected with a thorough forensic financial investigation. New York courts take asset concealment seriously and have systems in place to scrutinize financial behavior.

Consequences of Hiding Assets

If a court discovers that one spouse has hidden assets, the consequences can be severe. Not only could the concealed property be awarded entirely to the other spouse, but the dishonest party may also face fines, sanctions, or criminal charges.
