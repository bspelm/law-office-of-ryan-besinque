Understanding No-Fault Divorce Laws in New York

New York is a “no fault” divorce state, which means that couples can get a divorce if they claim that the marriage has irretrievably broken down and there is no hope of reconciliation. No-fault divorces are less contentious than fault-based divorces, as spouses are not required to present evidence and assign blame. However, there are a number of issues that must still be resolved, including property division, spousal support, and child custody. A qualified divorce lawyer NYS can provide guidance and support for the entire process, from filing to obtaining a final decree of divorce.

Until recently, New Yorkers seeking a divorce were required to establish one of the many legal grounds for divorce, such as adultery, cruel and inhuman treatment, abandonment or imprisonment. This was an expensive, emotionally taxing, and time-consuming approach to divorce and often made fault-based claims of wrongdoing a major focus of divorce proceedings. Fault-based divorces also influenced the outcome of property division and other financial aspects of a divorce.

In 2010, New York passed a no-fault divorce law that allows spouses to simply cite the irretrievable breakdown of the marriage as their reason for seeking a divorce, without having to prove fault or wrongdoing by either party. This change to the law frees up spouses to focus on the legal issues associated with a divorce, such as property division, alimony, and child custody.

While no fault divorces make the process less contentious, they do not eliminate some of the issues that must be resolved. For example, issues involving child custody, visitation, and support must be negotiated or decided by the court. In addition, a divorce will not be finalized until all economic issues (including debt and how to divide assets) are settled.
